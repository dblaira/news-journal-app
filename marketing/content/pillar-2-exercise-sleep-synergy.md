## Format B — LinkedIn Essay

**Title:** The single-lever fallacy: why exercise OR sleep alone doesn't work, and the 0.57 that does.

Four years of Apple Watch sleep data. Nineteen months of logged exercise. A daily record of how I actually felt. When I finally fed it through a personal ontology — a causal knowledge graph that looks at life domains the way AI research looks at variables — the result was the one axiom I thought I already understood, and did not.

Neither exercise alone nor sleep alone predicted excellent stress recovery in my data. Only the combination did.

The axiom: *If Exercise is high in a week AND Sleep is high in the same week, then stress recovery is excellent.* Confidence: 0.57. Either lever on its own was a weak signal — interesting, sometimes helpful, not reliable. The AND was the axiom.

This breaks the dominant pattern in self-improvement, which I would call the single-lever fallacy: the belief that if you fix *the one thing*, the rest will follow. "Prioritize sleep." "Start lifting." "Cut processed food." Each of these claims is individually useful and collectively misleading. The one thing is almost never the one thing. It is the specific combination with one other thing, in the same week, that moves the outcome.

Exercise without sleep: you recover slowly and blame the exercise. Sleep without exercise: you feel rested but flat. The two together: the week feels structurally different. Not 10% better. Different.

I did not discover this by journaling. Journaling — writing down what I noticed — was not the move. The move was recording specific entries tagged by life domain and letting a graph correlate Exercise-tagged weeks against Sleep-tagged weeks against a stress-recovery metric. The AND is invisible without the graph. Your intuition can name one-to-one relationships. It cannot see two-to-one relationships in time-series data. That is what the ontology is for.

Once the AND surfaces, it rearranges the instruction set. "Prioritize sleep" becomes "prioritize sleep *while exercising.*" That is a different instruction. It produces a different outcome.

The test, if you want to find your own 0.57: four days of exercise, seven nights of actual 7h-plus sleep, same week. Record Sunday night on a 1–10 how stress recovery felt. Run the test for three weeks. Three data points is not enough to calculate a real confidence, but it is enough to see the shape. If the shape shows up, you have your own version of the axiom. If it does not, you have learned something more valuable than a generic rule — you have learned that your ontology is different, and what to test next.
